Modeling/Predicting the Growth of Bike Sharing Services in DC¶

Wenxuan Yu

Introduction:¶

Bike-sharing services have been growing in popularity, as more people are choosing biking as their means of transportation, both increasing daily exercise, and decreasing our daily carbon footprint. This tutorial will explore data from the largest and the dominant bike-sharing service in DC, Capital Bikeshare. We will gain knowledge about peoples average ride time, the number of customers, the number of bikes, and more relevant data every year from 2010 to 2017 in order to picture the growth of the largest bike-sharing service in DC.

Because of the dominant role of Capital Bikeshare in the bike-sharing market, this model would reflect the overall growth of all bike-sharing services in DC. After having our 7 years of data cleaned up, we will use tools to predict the growth rate of the market and test our prediction using later years' data.

Data Collection:¶

The data we are using comes from the Capital Bikeshare database. The same data can be found at this link, under Trip History Data: https://ride.capitalbikeshare.com/system-data We are analyzing the data from 2010 to 2017 for this part of the tutorial.

The data includes these attributes:

  • Duration Duration of trip
  • Start Date Includes start date and time
  • End Date Includes end date and time
  • Start Station Includes starting station name and number
  • End Station Includes ending station name and number
  • Bike Number Includes ID number of bike used for the trip
  • Member Type Indicates whether the user was a "registered" member (Annual Member, 30-Day Member, or Day Key Member) or a "casual" rider (Single Trip, 24-Hour Pass, 3-Day Pass, or 5-Day Pass)

The data has been processed to remove trips that are taken by staff as they service and inspect the system, trips that are taken to/from any of the test stations at the Capital Bikeshare warehouses, and any trips lasting less than 60 seconds (potentially false starts or users trying to re-dock a bike to ensure it's secure).

In our case of bike-sharing data, the first thing we realize is our Start date and End date columns are not DateTime objects, which is a type of object that gives us easy access to the year, month, date, and time of the data. We will transform the two columns with pandas.to_datetime function.

After that, to identify which year is each row representing, we will extract the year from "Start date" column for every row, and create a new column showing us the year. This will also make actions like grouping data by year easier later. Finally, we will create new columns Day, "Hour", and Month to indicate the datas day of week, hour, and month. All these extra data will come in handy later when we try to analyze what day of the week people use bike-sharing services the most, for example, easier. We can always add more columns if needed later.

More information about DateTime can be found here: https://docs.python.org/3/library/datetime.html

More information about pandas.to_datetime can be found here: https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/reference/api/pandas.to_datetime.html

# We are converting 'Start date' and 'End date' columns to pandas DateTime objects. This way we can extract year, month, day, and time easily.
data_cp['Start date'] = pd.to_datetime(data_cp['Start date'])
data_cp['End date'] = pd.to_datetime(data_cp['End date'])
# Now we are adding a column called 'Year' to our DataFrame, so we can group our data by year easily.
data_cp['Year'] = pd.DatetimeIndex(data_cp['Start date']).year
# Adding the "Day" column indicating day-of-week
data_cp['Day'] = data['Start date'].dt.day_name()
# Adding the "Hour" column indicating hour of the day
data_cp['Hour'] = data['Start date'].dt.hour
# Adding the "Month" column indicating month
data_cp['Month'] = data['Start date'].dt.month
